Land Management · Reforestation

Greenpop Forests for Life Programme

South Africa — Community Reforestation Partnership

Type Community Reforestation
Partner Greenpop
Scale Multi-site, national
Focus Indigenous tree cover restoration
Model Community-led planting

Tree cover in South Africa has been significantly reduced by centuries of clearing, grazing, and development. The loss isn't just aesthetic — it's structural. Trees anchor soil, regulate water cycles, moderate temperature extremes, and create the habitat complexity that the rest of the food web depends on. Restoring tree cover at meaningful scale requires more than good intentions: it requires the right species, the right sites, and communities who understand why the trees matter and have a stake in their survival.

The Greenpop Partnership

Greenpop's Forests for Life programme is one of the more credible reforestation initiatives operating in South Africa — focused not just on planting numbers but on tree survival, community buy-in, and the ecological integrity of what gets planted. Six Kingdoms contributed ecological expertise to the programme: species selection based on local ecological context, site preparation guidance, and training for community planters in establishment techniques that give trees the best chance of survival in their first two critical seasons.

The partnership recognises a tension that undermines many reforestation programmes: the gap between counting trees planted and counting trees that are genuinely established and growing. A well-planted tree with appropriate mulching, watering in the establishment phase, and protection from grazing has a fundamentally different survival rate to a tree planted without any of those supports. The Forests for Life approach prioritises survival over headline planting figures.

Species Selection and Ecological Appropriateness

The most important decision in any reforestation project is which trees to plant. Indigenous species, matched to the local biome and soil conditions, are the only ecologically meaningful choice — they support the native fauna, fungi, and soil biology that exotic trees do not. Fast-growing exotic species may produce impressive coverage figures quickly, but they don't rebuild the ecological web.

For each site, species were selected based on a survey of remnant indigenous vegetation in the surrounding area — using the existing landscape as a guide to what belongs there. This approach also improves outcomes: locally adapted species from the right provenance outperform transplanted material from distant nurseries, even when the species are the same.

"We planted with the community and then came back a season later. The trees were alive, the people knew why they mattered, and some had already planted more on their own initiative."

Establishment and Community Training

Tree planting events are only meaningful if the trees survive. Establishment support — watering schedules, mulching depth, protection from herbivore browsing, and recognition of early stress signs — was built into the programme as embedded training for each community involved. The goal was to leave communities with the knowledge to maintain the trees without ongoing external support, and to extend planting onto their own land where conditions allowed.

Key Details

  • Multi-site reforestation across degraded South African landscapes in partnership with Greenpop
  • Indigenous species only — selected from surveys of local remnant vegetation
  • Community training in establishment techniques — mulching, watering, protection
  • Survival tracking built into the programme from the outset
  • Measurably higher survival rates than standard reforestation approaches
