Knysna New-Build EcoPool

Knysna, Garden Route — Fynbos Slope Site

Type New Build EcoPool
Location Knysna, Garden Route
Site character Fynbos slope
Water source Rainwater harvesting
Chemicals used None. Ever.

Building a new natural pool from scratch on a Knysna fynbos slope is one of the more rewarding briefs to work with. The site already had the right bones: indigenous vegetation, a natural slope to work with for passive water movement, the kind of ecology that actually wants to participate in what you're building. The challenge is designing a pool that slots into that context rather than displacing it.

Starting From Scratch

Unlike a conversion, a new build gives you the freedom to design from first principles — the right size, the right ratio of swim zone to regeneration zone, the right aspect and orientation for the biology to perform well. On this Knysna property, the fynbos context also meant that the pool could be connected to a broader garden strategy: the planted zone was designed to extend visually into the surrounding indigenous planting, so from a distance the pool reads as part of the landscape rather than an installation sitting on top of it.

The slope was an asset. On sloped sites, passive water circulation between swim zone and regeneration zone can be driven partly by gravity — reducing pump dependence and the energy footprint of the system. The design took advantage of that by positioning the regeneration zone at the slope's natural catchment point.

Indigenous Aquatic Planting

The regeneration zone was planted with species chosen for their proven biological filtration capacity and their compatibility with the Knysna fynbos ecology. Using locally appropriate species matters for more than aesthetics — native aquatic plants are adapted to local water chemistry, seasonal rainfall patterns, and the temperature range of the region. They perform better, establish faster, and don't introduce the ecological risk that exotic species sometimes carry.

Species established in the regeneration zone include submerged oxygenators, emergent restios adapted to wet conditions, indigenous sedges, and a selection of bog-zone plants that create the transitional zone between open water and dry land. This gradient of planting types creates a functioning ecology rather than just a row of water plants.

"The pool was swimming beautifully from the first season. No chemistry, no complications — just a pool that works the way water is supposed to."

Rainwater Integration

This pool was designed from the outset to be primarily rainwater fed — rather than drawing from a mains supply, it relies on the site's own rainfall catchment supplemented by roof rainwater collection. On the Knysna portion of the Garden Route, annual rainfall is generally sufficient to keep a well-designed natural pool at level without supplementary filling, particularly during the winter wet season.

Designing for rainwater feeding also means designing a pool that can handle the variable chemistry of rainwater — slightly softer and more acidic than mains supply, which suits the fynbos-adapted aquatic species chosen for this site.

Key Details

  • New build designed from first principles for a Garden Route fynbos slope context
  • Slope used for passive water circulation — reduced pump dependence
  • Indigenous aquatic species throughout — all locally appropriate for the Knysna ecology
  • Primary water source: rainwater harvesting from roof and site catchment
  • Regeneration zone designed to merge visually with the surrounding fynbos planting
  • Swimming well from the end of the first season; biology fully established by year two
