Fynbos Restoration: Reading a Landscape Back to Life

February 2026 — 5 min read

Every landscape has a story written in its soil, its plants, and the way water moves across it. When we first walked this site near Plettenberg Bay, it took a while to read. Years of invasive plant encroachment had obscured most of the original fynbos understorey, and the drainage patterns had been disrupted by old earthworks. But the bones were still there. Our job was to start uncovering them.

The First Walk

We never start a project by deciding what to plant. We start by looking. What's already here? Where does water pool after rain, and where does it race off too fast? What's the aspect — which slopes face north, baking in summer, and which stay cool and damp? What's coming back on its own where the land has been left alone? The answers to those questions shape everything that follows.

This particular site had several things going for it: a remnant fynbos seed bank that we could see in patches where the kikuyu and wattle hadn't completely taken over, a natural drainage line running through the lower portion of the property, and owners who understood that restoration is a long-term project, not a one-season fix.

The best restoration work happens when the landowner is genuinely curious about their land, not just wanting it to look a certain way. That curiosity is what drives the patience restoration requires.

Dealing with the Invasives First

You can't restore fynbos into a site still dominated by invasive alien plants. The first phase of work was methodical clearing — working from the least-invaded areas outward, removing wattle, rooikrans, and hakea using hand tools wherever possible to minimise soil disturbance. Disturbed soil is an open invitation for weed reinfestation, so we work carefully.

Wattle stumps were treated to prevent resprouting. The cut material was processed on-site where possible — chipped and used as mulch rather than burned or removed, which would have taken nutrients off the property and left bare soil exposed.

This phase takes longer than most people expect. There's a temptation to clear everything quickly and get to the planting, but rushing it sets you up for failure. The aliens come back. You have to stay ahead of them, especially in the first two years before the indigenous vegetation is dense enough to compete.

Designing the Rain Garden

The drainage line was an opportunity. Rather than channelling water off the property as quickly as possible — the conventional approach — we redesigned it as a series of connected rain gardens: shallow, planted basins that catch and slow stormwater, allowing it to infiltrate rather than run off. In a fynbos context, this is particularly valuable. Fynbos is adapted to nutrient-poor, well-drained soils, but it also depends on reliable soil moisture through the dry season — slow infiltration recharges this more effectively than fast-moving runoff.

The rain gardens were planted with a mix of restios, rushes, and sedges in the wetter zones, with ericas and proteas on the margins where it drains more freely. Local bulbous species — oxalis, gladiolus, watsonia — fill in the spaces between, adding colour through the seasons and feeding the pollinators that the fynbos insects depend on.

Species selection

On the Garden Route, you're working with one of the most biodiverse floras on earth. That's an extraordinary resource, but it also means that getting species selection wrong is easy. Fynbos is highly localised — what thrives on a sandstone ridge in Knysna may struggle on a shale slope near Plett. We source plants locally wherever possible, and we lean heavily on what's already naturally recruiting on the site itself as our guide to what belongs there.

Where It's Heading

Restoration is measured in seasons, not weeks. After the first clearing and planting phase, the work becomes observation and management — monitoring what's coming back, staying on top of any alien regrowth, adjusting the planting plan where something isn't establishing as expected. It takes patience. But watching a landscape find its own rhythm again — the birds returning, the proteas flowering, the first small frog appearing in the rain garden — is one of the more satisfying things we get to do.
