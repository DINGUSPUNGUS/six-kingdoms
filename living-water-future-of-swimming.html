EcoPools

Why Living Water is the Future of Swimming

March 2026 — 4 min read

Conventional pools are a constant argument with water. You dose it with chlorine to kill algae, add algaecide when chlorine isn't enough, balance the pH, shock the system when something goes wrong, and repeat. The chemistry never quite stays settled. An EcoPool starts from a different premise entirely: instead of fighting the water, you let it do what water does when it's healthy — filter itself.

What Actually Keeps the Water Clean

A natural swimming pool works through a regeneration zone — a planted section of the pool, physically separated from the swimming area, where aquatic plants and beneficial micro-organisms break down organic matter and outcompete algae for the nutrients it needs to thrive. The biology does what chlorine does, but without the side effects.

The plants aren't decorative. They're functional. Reeds, water irises, and other aquatic species act as a living filter, absorbing nitrogen and phosphorus that would otherwise feed algae. Below the surface, a layer of fine gravel hosts the microbial communities that break down organic material at the molecular level. Together, they create a closed-loop system that becomes more stable and self-regulating over time — not less.

"We'd been putting the pool off for two years because we didn't want the chemicals. The EcoPool sorted that. The frogs moved in by April." — David & Lisa Thompson, Knysna

What It Means to Swim in Living Water

The difference is immediate. Chlorinated water is hard on your body — it strips natural oils from skin and hair, irritates eyes, and leaves that chemical smell that signals to your immune system that something's off. Natural pool water is soft. It doesn't sting. It doesn't smell. Your skin feels better after a swim than before it.

There's also something harder to quantify: the experience of swimming in water that's alive. A dragonfly landing on the edge. A frog that's made a home in the reeds. Kingfishers hunting the margins. An EcoPool doesn't just give you a place to swim — it adds something to the garden that wasn't there before.

Why the Garden Route is Ideal for EcoPools

Natural pools work everywhere, but they work especially well here. The Garden Route's mild, temperate climate means aquatic plants grow vigorously for most of the year. The region's indigenous flora — particularly the fynbos and renosterveld species adapted to local rainfall patterns — integrate naturally into the planting palette. And the local ecology is genuinely interested in water: the biodiversity that colonises a well-designed EcoPool on the Garden Route is remarkable.

We've built pools in Knysna, Plettenberg Bay, and across the valleys between them. Each one has its own character, shaped by the site it sits on. That's one of the things we find most satisfying about this work — no two are alike.

The Practical Side

EcoPools do require some maintenance, but it's different in kind from conventional pool maintenance. There are no chemicals to buy or handle. The main ongoing tasks are managing plant growth in the regeneration zone and occasionally skimming debris from the swimming area. A well-established EcoPool typically takes less time to maintain than a chlorine pool — and the work is more gardening than chemistry.

The upfront build cost is comparable to a well-constructed conventional pool. Running costs are lower. And unlike a chlorine pool, an EcoPool tends to improve with age rather than degrade — the biology becomes richer and more balanced as the system matures.
