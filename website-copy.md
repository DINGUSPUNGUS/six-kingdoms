## HOME > FAQ SECTION TITLE
Questions People Usually Ask

## HOME > FAQ 1 — QUESTION
What is an EcoPool?

## HOME > FAQ 1 — ANSWER
A swimming pool without chemicals. Instead of chlorine, an EcoPool has a planted regeneration zone — aquatic plants and beneficial micro-organisms do the filtering work. The water is clean, soft on skin, and stays remarkably clear. Things also actually live in it, which is half the appeal.

## HOME > FAQ 2 — QUESTION
How are natural swimming pools different from conventional pools?

## HOME > FAQ 2 — ANSWER
The short version: no chemicals, ever. No chlorine burning your eyes, no algaecide top-ups, no chemical management schedule. Once the biology is established it's largely self-regulating. They also just look better — a natural pool sits in the garden rather than sitting on top of it.

## HOME > FAQ 3 — QUESTION
What areas do you service?

## HOME > FAQ 3 — ANSWER
Mostly the Garden Route — Plettenberg Bay, Knysna, George, Wilderness, and the surrounding areas. For the right project we'll travel further, but the Garden Route is home base.

## HOME > FAQ 4 — QUESTION
What does ecological design involve?

## HOME > FAQ 4 — ANSWER
Mostly, it means slowing down and reading a site before you do anything to it. Where does the water go when it rains? What's already growing? What should be growing? Then you design with those patterns rather than against them. The result is a space that needs less from you over time, because it's working with the place instead of fighting it.

## HOME > FAQ 5 — QUESTION
Do you offer ongoing maintenance services?

## HOME > FAQ 5 — ANSWER
Yes — and it's honestly some of our favourite work. We'd rather help you understand your land than just maintain it for you indefinitely. So the ongoing support is usually a mix of hands-on visits and helping you learn what to look for yourself.

## HOME > FAQ 6 — QUESTION
How do I get started?

## HOME > FAQ 6 — ANSWER
Just drop us a message. We'll arrange a time to talk through what you're dealing with — your site, what you're hoping for, your timeline. From there we'll tell you what makes sense. No obligation.

## LIVING WATER > PAGE TITLE
Why Living Water is the Future of Swimming | Six Kingdoms Blog

## LIVING WATER > META DESCRIPTION
Why natural swimming pools — filtered by biology, not chemicals — are a healthier, more sustainable choice for Garden Route homeowners.

## LIVING WATER > TAG
EcoPools

## LIVING WATER > HEADLINE
Why Living Water is the Future of Swimming

## LIVING WATER > DATE
March 2026

## LIVING WATER > READ TIME
4 min read

## LIVING WATER > INTRO PARAGRAPH
A swimming pool should feel like swimming. Not like a sanitised tank. Yet the vast majority of pools built today use the same chemistry as drinking water disinfection — chlorine, algaecides, pH adjusters — fought constantly against the pool's natural tendency to become something alive. EcoPools work with that tendency rather than against it, and the result is a fundamentally different swimming experience.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 1 HEADING
What Makes Water "Living"?

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 1
In any healthy waterway — a river, a wetland, a clean mountain pool — the water stays clear not because it's chemically treated, but because of what lives in it. Beneficial bacteria break down organic matter. Aquatic plants absorb nutrients. Micro-organisms maintain a biological balance that simply doesn't allow algae or pathogens to dominate. The system self-regulates.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 2
An EcoPool recreates this system deliberately. A dedicated regeneration zone — planted with indigenous aquatic species, seeded with beneficial micro-organisms, and designed to the specific hydrology of your site — does the filtration work. The swimming zone stays clean because the biology works. There is no chlorine added, no chemicals managed, and no ongoing chemical cost.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 2 HEADING
What's Wrong with Chlorine?

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 2 PARAGRAPH 1
Chlorinated pools are not neutral. Chlorine irritates eyes and skin, bleaches hair, and degrades swimwear. People with sensitive skin, eczema, or respiratory conditions frequently report problems. Children swimming regularly in chlorinated pools have measurable exposure to disinfection byproducts — compounds formed when chlorine reacts with organic matter — which are increasingly the subject of health research.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 2 PARAGRAPH 2
Beyond human health, chlorinated water that splashes onto garden beds, runs off during cleaning, or is drained for maintenance damages soil biology and kills the microorganisms that healthy soil depends on. On the Garden Route, where fynbos ecology is both spectacular and fragile, that matters.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 2 BLOCKQUOTE
"The water is mineral-rich, soft on skin, and alive in a way that a chlorinated pool never is. By the second summer, dragonflies were laying eggs along the edge."

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 3 HEADING
The Garden Route Case

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 3 PARAGRAPH 1
The Western Cape receives reliable winter rainfall. The Garden Route is one of the most biodiverse regions in Africa. Indigenous aquatic species — the plants, insects, and micro-organisms that populate healthy water bodies in this region — are exceptionally well-suited to populating a natural pool's regeneration zone. The ecology here doesn't need to be coaxed; it wants to participate.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 3 PARAGRAPH 2
We've built EcoPools in Knysna, Plettenberg Bay, and across the Garden Route valleys. Every one has gone through the same process: a slow initial establishment phase where biology colonises the system, followed by a tipping point — usually within one season — where the pool finds its equilibrium and simply works. After that, maintenance is minimal. No chemicals to buy, no pH to monitor, no algaecide cycles. The biology does it.

## LIVING WATER > SUBSECTION 1 HEADING
Running Costs

## LIVING WATER > SUBSECTION 1 PARAGRAPH
Chlorinated pools require ongoing chemical purchase, typically automated dosing systems, and professional water testing. A well-designed EcoPool has lower electricity requirements (the pump runs at lower duty cycles), no chemical costs, and longer equipment lifespan because the system isn't fighting corrosion from chlorine compounds.

## LIVING WATER > RUNNING COSTS LIST
- No chemical purchase costs
- Lower pump running hours
- No automated dosing equipment to maintain
- Longer liner and equipment lifespan
- No drainage and refill cycles

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 4 HEADING
Is a Natural Pool Right for Your Property?

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 4 PARAGRAPH 1
EcoPools work best on properties with adequate space for a regeneration zone — typically 30–50% of the total pool area. They need some natural light for the planted zone to function. On very shaded or very small sites, a conventional system may be more appropriate. We're honest about this from the start. If a natural pool isn't the right fit for your site, we'll say so rather than oversell the concept.

## LIVING WATER > SECTION 4 PARAGRAPH 2
For most Garden Route properties — the garden-scale homes in Knysna, the larger lifestyle plots around Plettenberg Bay and George, the smallholdings in the valleys — an EcoPool is not only possible but usually the better long-term choice. It becomes part of the garden ecology rather than a separate maintenance obligation.

## FYNBOS > PAGE TITLE
Fynbos Restoration: Reading a Landscape Back to Life | Six Kingdoms Blog

## FYNBOS > META DESCRIPTION
How Six Kingdoms approaches fynbos restoration and rain garden integration on a Garden Route property near Plettenberg Bay.

## FYNBOS > TAG
Ecological Design

## FYNBOS > HEADLINE
Fynbos Restoration: Reading a Landscape Back to Life

## FYNBOS > DATE
February 2026

## FYNBOS > READ TIME
5 min read

## FYNBOS > INTRO PARAGRAPH
Fynbos doesn't need much help. It's one of the most species-rich plant biomes on Earth, evolved over millions of years for exactly the conditions found along South Africa's Cape coast — the thin, nutrient-poor soils, the dry summers, the fire cycles, the winter rain. What fynbos needs is for the things working against it to be removed. That's where design comes in.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 1 HEADING
The Site: Near Plettenberg Bay

## FYNBOS > SECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 1
The property we worked on sits on a south-facing slope above the coast near Plettenberg Bay — around two hectares of largely degraded land that had been cleared, overgrazed, and partially colonised by invasive alien species over the previous decade. The original fynbos had retreated to the margins. The soil surface was compacted in places, and without functioning plant cover, the winter rain was eroding material off the slope rather than infiltrating it.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 2
The brief from the landowner was broad: "make it look like it should." That's actually a more precise brief than it sounds. What should it look like? Fynbos. How does fynbos work? You don't plant it so much as reinstate the conditions under which it re-establishes itself.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 2 HEADING
Reading the Landscape

## FYNBOS > SECTION 2 PARAGRAPH
Before we touch anything, we spend time on site. We walk it in different weather. We look at where water moves, where it pools, where the soil is deepest and where it's thin. We look at what's already growing — even weedy, degraded vegetation tells you something about the underlying ecology. The remnant fynbos patches on this property were small but intact, and they told us which species were locally appropriate and where the soil conditions could support what we were trying to reinstate.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 2 BLOCKQUOTE
Fynbos restoration is fundamentally an act of removal. You take out the things that are suppressing the local ecology, and then you get out of the way.

## FYNBOS > SUBSECTION 1 HEADING
Invasive alien clearing

## FYNBOS > SUBSECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 1
The primary intervention was invasive alien plant removal. Rooikrans (Acacia cyclops), black wattle (Acacia mearnsii), and Port Jackson willow had established on the lower slope and around the drainage lines. These species are highly competitive, fix nitrogen in ways that alter fynbos-adapted soils, and suppress the light conditions that fynbos seedlings require. Clearing was done manually — cut and treat methods to minimise soil disturbance, which itself can trigger weed regrowth.

## FYNBOS > SUBSECTION 1 PARAGRAPH 2
This phase is unglamorous but foundational. A fynbos restoration that skips or rushes the invasive clearing phase will not hold. The alien species will re-establish faster than the fynbos can compete.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 3 HEADING
The Rain Garden

## FYNBOS > SECTION 3 PARAGRAPH 1
The second component of this project was integrating a rain garden into the lower part of the slope — a designed depression planted with water-tolerant indigenous species that captures and infiltrates stormwater runoff before it reaches the property boundary. This serves several functions at once: it slows and sinks water that would otherwise erode the slope, it creates a different microhabitat within the broader restoration area, and it recharges the shallow groundwater table that fynbos roots depend on during dry summers.

## FYNBOS > SECTION 3 PARAGRAPH 2
The species palette was drawn from indigenous wetland and streamside plants native to the Garden Route — Cape restio reeds, water hawthorn, indigenous sedges — chosen for their ability to thrive in seasonally wet conditions and to support local insect and bird populations. A rain garden is not a pond. It drains between rain events. But for a few days after significant rain, it holds water, and in that time it does important ecological work.

## FYNBOS > SUBSECTION 2 HEADING
What "success" looks like

## FYNBOS > SUBSECTION 2 PARAGRAPH 1
Six months on, the site is showing what we hoped for. The cleared areas are regenerating from soil-stored seed bank — species we didn't plant, emerging because the conditions were right. The rain garden held water through the last winter rain events and is already attracting Cape sugarbirds and malachite sunbirds drawn to the restio seed heads. The erosion on the lower slope has measurably slowed.
