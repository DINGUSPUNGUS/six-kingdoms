Land Management

Knowing Your Land: The Foundation
of Good Stewardship

January 2026 — 6 min read

Most land management problems are intervention problems. Something has gone wrong — invasive species spreading, soil eroding, water running off rather than infiltrating, biodiversity declining — and the instinct is to act quickly. Buy the herbicide, hire the contractor, implement the plan. The observation phase gets skipped. This is usually why the problem continues.

What Observation Actually Means

Spending time on a piece of land before intervening isn't passive. It's one of the most information-dense things you can do. Where does water move during rain? Where does it pool? Where are the soil conditions visibly different — darker, lighter, more compacted? Which areas are the invasive plants advancing from, and which direction are they moving? What is already growing that's native, however sparse or suppressed?

These questions can't be answered from a desktop assessment or a single site visit. They require time across seasons and weather events. On the Garden Route, this means observing a site through at least one significant rain event, through the dry summer, and ideally watching what regenerates after fire or disturbance. The land is always communicating its condition. Learning to read it is what separates land management that holds from land management that has to be repeated every two years.

The Holistic Context

The methodology we use draws on Holistic Planned Grazing and the broader framework of holistic land management developed by Allan Savory and the Savory Institute. The core of this approach is what Savory calls the "holistic context" — a clear description of what you want the land to become, who it needs to support, and what the quality of life you're designing for looks like. Without this context, every management decision becomes arbitrary.

You can't manage what you don't understand. And you can't understand land by looking at it from a vehicle.

For a smallholder in the Garden Route valleys, the holistic context might look like: productive food systems, recovering indigenous vegetation on the slopes, clean water from the stream on the lower boundary, a property that increases in ecological value rather than declining over time. Every intervention is then tested against that context. Does this action move us toward it, or away?

Invasive Species: The Long View

Invasive alien plant management is one of the most common tasks we're brought in for — and one of the most mismanaged. The default approach is chemical: foliar spray, cut-and-treat, blanket applications. These methods work in specific contexts but routinely fail when applied without understanding the underlying reasons for the invasion.

Invasive species colonise disturbed, degraded, or destabilised soil. The disturbance creates the niche. If you clear the invasives without addressing what created the disturbance — whether it's overgrazing, compaction, erosion, altered hydrology, or simply bare soil after a construction project — the invasives will return. You've cleared the symptom, not the condition.

What this looks like in practice

On a project near George, we were asked to manage a wattle infestation that had returned three times despite previous clearing. The previous contractor had been thorough. The problem was that the clearing work — mechanised, and done when the soil was wet — had created significant compaction and left large areas of bare mineral soil. Within one growing season, the bare soil had been colonised by wattle seedlings from the soil seed bank.

Our approach was to address the compaction and bare soil first — using cover cropping, compost, and grazing to restore surface cover — before the next clearing cycle. The wattle was still treated, but into established groundcover rather than bare soil. Eighteen months later, the regrowth rate is dramatically lower than the previous three cycles.

  • Identify the underlying cause of invasion, not just the invasive itself
  • Restore soil cover before or alongside clearing
  • Time clearing to avoid creating bare soil in high-risk periods
  • Follow up is non-negotiable — one clearance is never the end
  • Monitor regeneration of indigenous species as the primary success indicator

Water: The Central Resource

On every land management project we undertake, water management is foundational. The Garden Route has reliable rainfall, but that's not the same as having reliable soil moisture. Rain that runs off doesn't benefit the land. Rain that infiltrates slowly through functional soil, is held in the profile, and recharges the shallow water table — that's what builds resilience.

Practical water harvesting — contour swales, retention dams, stone lines on slopes — can transform a property's water dynamics without significant infrastructure. On a degraded slope, a well-placed swale breaks the run-off pathway and causes water to spread and sink rather than concentrate into erosion channels. These are low-cost, high-leverage interventions that pay compound dividends as the infiltration builds soil life and enables native vegetation establishment.

If there's a single message in land stewardship, it's this: start with water, start with observation, and work with what the land is trying to do. The results are slower to arrive than a bulldozer and a tank of herbicide. But they last.
