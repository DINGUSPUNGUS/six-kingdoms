Salt River Invasive Plant Clearing

Salt River, Cape Town — Riverine Habitat Restoration

Type Invasive Plant Clearing
Location Salt River, Cape Town
Habitat Urban riverine corridor
Scale Large-scale clearing
Approach Clearing + indigenous replanting

Urban rivers are under enormous ecological pressure. Invaded by non-native plants, cut off from the broader landscape by roads and development, and often degraded to the point where the water quality and hydrological function of the corridor are both compromised. The Salt River corridor in Cape Town is typical of this challenge: a functioning urban watercourse that, with the right intervention, has significant ecological value — and that without it, continues to deteriorate.

The Problem with Invasive Aliens in Riparian Zones

Riparian zones — the bands of vegetation alongside rivers — are among the most ecologically important habitats in any landscape. They stabilise banks, filter runoff before it reaches the water, provide critical wildlife corridors, moderate water temperature through shading, and support a disproportionately high diversity of species. When invasive alien plants take over a riparian zone, they undermine all of these functions.

Species like black wattle, Port Jackson willow, and blue gum dominate the Salt River corridor in the invaded sections. These are heavy water users — they draw massively from shallow water tables, reducing stream flow during dry seasons. Their dense canopy shades out native understorey plants. Their leaf litter changes soil chemistry in ways that make it harder for indigenous plants to re-establish. And their root systems can alter bank structure in ways that increase erosion risk.

The Clearing Programme

The clearing work was structured around a compartmental approach — dividing the affected section of corridor into manageable zones and clearing systematically, with the most strategically important sections prioritised first. Clearing was done using a combination of cut-stump treatment and foliar application depending on species and life stage — the method chosen for each stand based on what would minimise re-sprouting and chemical use simultaneously.

Alien plant clearing in riverine environments requires particular care around chemical use — the proximity of the river means that herbicide choice and application method are critical to avoid waterway contamination. The programme used registered herbicides at label rates and avoided any application within the immediate riparian zone where contamination risk was highest.

"The change in the cleared zones is already visible. You can see the indigenous plants coming back on their own where the wattle has been removed."

Replanting and Follow-Up

Clearing alone is not enough — cleared zones fill with whatever seedbank is available, and in heavily invaded areas that seedbank is often dominated by alien species. The programme included targeted replanting of the cleared zones with indigenous riparian species selected for their bank-stabilising root systems, their value for wildlife, and their establishment speed to outcompete alien regrowth.

Follow-up clearing of re-sprouts and new seedlings was scheduled into the ongoing management plan — alien clearing without a follow-up programme typically results in re-invasion within 2-3 years. The maintenance schedule ensures that the progress made in the initial clearing phase is consolidated and built on rather than unwound.

Key Details

  • Urban riverine corridor — Salt River, Cape Town
  • Primary invasives: black wattle, Port Jackson willow, blue gum
  • Compartmental clearing approach — priority zones first
  • Herbicide use minimised and carefully managed near water
  • Riparian replanting with indigenous bank-stabilising species
  • Structured follow-up maintenance schedule to prevent re-invasion
