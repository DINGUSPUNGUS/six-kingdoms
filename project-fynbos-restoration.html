Ecological Design

Fynbos Restoration & Rain Garden Design

Plettenberg Bay, Garden Route

Category Ecological Design
Location Plettenberg Bay, Garden Route
Services Fynbos Restoration, Rain Garden

This project began with a site that had real potential but a real problem: runoff. A property on the edges of Plettenberg Bay with beautiful remnant fynbos on its upper slopes, but years of poor drainage had degraded the lower ground — leaving compacted soil, erosion gullies, and a patchy mix of invasive and indigenous plants fighting for the same space.

Reading the Site

Before touching anything, we spent time walking the land through a full rain event — watching where the water moved, where it pooled, where it cut channels, and where it simply ran away fast. That observation shapes everything. Most drainage problems are solved downstream when the real fix is to slow water at the source.

The upper section had strong fynbos species still coming through — restios, ericas, and proteaceous shrubs holding their own against the kikuyu encroachment. The lower section needed the most work: invasive rooikrans had established a foothold and the soil structure had suffered.

The Rain Garden Design

A rain garden is a shallow, planted depression designed to absorb and filter stormwater runoff from impervious surfaces like roofs, driveways, and compacted paths. On this site we integrated a series of linked swales and gardens into the lower slope — each planted with sedges, restios, and wet-tolerant fynbos species that love having their feet in periodic water.

"The rain garden didn't just fix the drainage — it turned what was a problem area into the most interesting corner of the property."

The planted zones act as sponges during heavy rainfall, allowing water to filter slowly into the ground rather than rushing off the property and taking topsoil with it. By the second wet season, those areas had transformed — root systems had established, the soil had loosened, and water movement across the whole site had noticeably slowed and spread.

Fynbos Restoration

On the upper slopes, the work was mostly about removing what shouldn't be there so what should be could come back on its own. Kikuyu was hand-cleared and smothered with mulch. Rooikrans was cut and stump-treated. Then we waited — and the fynbos responded.

A small programme of direct seeding and plugging helped fill gaps, using seed collected from the site itself where possible. Local provenance matters in fynbos. These plants are adapted to this exact combination of soil chemistry, rainfall pattern, and fire regime — they're not interchangeable with specimens sourced from 50km away.

  • Invasive rooikrans and kikuyu cleared across approximately 0.8ha
  • Rain garden network designed across the lower slope
  • Restios, ericas, and wet-tolerant fynbos species established in the drainage zones
  • Locally-sourced seed used for direct fynbos restoration on upper slopes
  • Follow-up site visits in Year 1 and Year 2 to assess regrowth and manage any reinvasion

Two Years On

The last visit to this site showed what good ecological design does over time — it becomes less visible. The rain gardens are now barely distinguishable from the surrounding planting. The fynbos is thickening up. The drainage problem isn't something anyone talks about anymore because it isn't a problem. That's the outcome we're after.
