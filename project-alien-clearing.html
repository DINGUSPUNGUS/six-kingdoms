Invasive Alien Plant Clearing

Garden Route & Western Cape — Ongoing IAP Management

Type Invasive Alien Plant Clearing
Region Garden Route & Western Cape
Target species Wattle, Hakea, Pine, Port Jackson
Method Cut-stump, hack-and-squirt, follow-up
Outcome Indigenous recovery

South Africa's invasive alien plant problem is one of the most significant conservation challenges the country faces. Wattles, hakeas, pines, Port Jackson willows, and a catalogue of other introduced species occupy millions of hectares of land that was once fynbos, renosterveld, riparian forest, or grassland. They don't just occupy the space — they actively change it: altering soil chemistry, intercepting rainfall, reducing water table levels, and creating fire conditions that are far more intense than indigenous vegetation would produce.

More Than Just Cutting

The most common mistake in alien clearing is treating it as a one-off event. Cut a wattle, leave the stump, come back in two years to find twenty coppice shoots where there was one stem. Effective invasive plant management is a programme, not a single intervention: initial clearing, correct stump treatment, follow-up visits to catch regrowth and seedling germination, and finally — once the invasives are genuinely suppressed — supporting the recovery of the indigenous species that should be there.

We approach alien clearing as integrated land management. The goal isn't just a cleared patch — it's a patch that stays cleared and eventually transitions to functional indigenous vegetation. That requires understanding the seed bank in the soil (particularly for wattles, which can persist for decades), planning the clearing sequence to minimise disturbance and avoid creating erosion-prone bare ground, and knowing which indigenous species are likely to establish naturally versus where active replanting is needed.

Riparian Clearing

Riparian zones — the strips of land along rivers and streams — are often the most heavily invaded areas on a property, and also the most ecologically valuable ones. Water availability favours invasive riparian species like Port Jackson willow and various wattle species. But clearing riparian invasives recovers enormous water yield: research in South Africa consistently shows that removing riparian invasives increases stream flow, sometimes dramatically. This is one of the clearest demonstrations that alien clearing pays for itself in restored water resources, particularly relevant on properties with watercourses.

"We hadn't seen clear water in that stream in years. Within one winter season of clearing the wattles upstream, it was running clean and the frogs were back."

The Follow-Up Is Non-Negotiable

Initial clearing without follow-up is money spent with limited long-term outcome. Wattle seed banks in South African soils can germinate prolifically for 15 to 25 years after clearing — the seed was there long before you cleared. The follow-up programme — visiting treated areas at six to twelve month intervals, treating regrowth and seedlings at the right stage — is what converts initial clearing investment into genuine, lasting recovery. We build follow-up programmes into all our alien clearing contracts.

Key Details

  • Full clearing programme — initial clearing, stump treatment, and structured follow-up schedule
  • Riparian clearing with water yield recovery as a measurable outcome
  • Seed bank management — long-term seedling treatment to prevent reinvasion
  • Target species: Acacia mearnsii & cyclops (wattles), hakea, pinus species, Port Jackson willow
  • Method selection based on species, slope, and adjacent sensitive vegetation
  • Post-clearing indigenous recovery monitoring and replanting where required
