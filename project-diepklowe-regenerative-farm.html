Land Management · Regenerative Agriculture

Diepklowe Regenerative Farm

Western Cape, South Africa — Whole-Farm Regenerative Transition

Type Regenerative Farm Transition
Location Western Cape, South Africa
Scale Working farm
Approach Holistic Land Management
Focus Soil health & water retention

Farms that have been conventionally managed for decades carry the accumulated costs of that management in their soil: compaction, reduced biology, declining organic matter, and topsoil that no longer holds water the way it once did. The transition to regenerative practice isn't a switch you flip — it's a process of working with the land to gradually rebuild what was lost, while keeping a working farm operational throughout.

The Diepklowe Context

Diepklowe is a working farm that came to us looking not for a wholesale revolution but for a credible, practical pathway toward regenerative practice. The owners had been aware for some time that the land wasn't performing as it once had — yields were harder to maintain, water was running off rather than sinking in after rain, and the diversity of bird and insect life on the property had declined noticeably over the years. They wanted to understand what was happening and what they could do about it without putting the farm economics at risk.

The engagement began with a thorough site assessment — soil sampling across different paddocks and land use zones, vegetation surveys, a water flow analysis during a rainfall event, and an honest audit of what management practices had been contributing to the observed degradation.

Soil Health as the Foundation

Soil biology is the engine of a healthy farm. In a regenerative system, soil biology drives nutrient cycling, water infiltration, plant resilience, and carbon storage — but that biology has to be present and functioning. On farms that have been tilled regularly, treated with synthetic fertilisers, or heavily grazed without rest periods, the biology is often depleted. Rebuilding it is a multi-season process, but the direction of change is visible within the first season when management shifts.

At Diepklowe, we worked with the owners to implement cover cropping on rested paddocks, introduce planned grazing rotations that allow adequate recovery periods between grazing events, and stop tillage on the paddocks being transitioned. Compost tea applications were used to inoculate compacted areas with fungal and bacterial life, accelerating the recovery of soil biology.

Water: Slow It, Spread It, Sink It

The farm's water problem was fundamentally a question of soil structure. Water that doesn't sink into healthy, structured soil runs off — taking topsoil with it and leaving the land drier than it would otherwise be. As the soil biology begins to recover and organic matter builds up, water infiltration improves. Earthworks — keyline subsoiling and some strategic swale work on the steeper slopes — were used to accelerate this process by physically slowing runoff and directing it across the landscape rather than channelling it off.

"We started noticing the difference by the second winter. The water was staying in the ground longer. The grass was recovering faster after grazing. And there were birds back in the paddocks that we hadn't seen in years."

Key Details

  • Whole-farm regenerative transition — soil biology, water, grazing, and vegetation
  • Planned grazing rotations replacing continuous grazing
  • Cover cropping on transitioning paddocks
  • Keyline subsoiling and swale earthworks for water retention
  • Compost tea applications for soil biology inoculation
  • Measurable improvement in water infiltration and pasture recovery within two seasons
