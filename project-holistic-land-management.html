Land Management

A Vision for Holistic Land Management

Garden Route, South Africa

Category Land Management
Location Garden Route, South Africa
Services Site Assessment, Holistic Planning, Stewardship

Most land management projects don't start with a plan — they start with a question. Sometimes it's "what is this stuff taking over the back boundary?" Sometimes it's "we want to do something with this land but we don't know what." This project was the latter. A property on the Garden Route with significant acreage, a mix of degraded and intact vegetation, and owners who were committed to doing something meaningful but weren't sure where to begin.

Starting with What's There

The first step was a full day on-site — walking every part of the property and taking stock. Not just of what plants were there, but of how water moved across the land, where soils were compacted or eroded, which areas were recovering on their own, and which needed active intervention. We look for the land's existing momentum: places where nature is already trying to do something, and how we can help rather than work against it.

This site had a healthy remnant patch of indigenous forest along a drainage line in the eastern corner. That became the reference point for everything else — a demonstration of what the land could do, and a seed bank of species that could spread outward if given the right conditions.

Building the Vision Together

A holistic land management vision isn't a document we hand over — it's something built in conversation. The owners understood this land better than any specialist ever would. What they lacked was a framework for making decisions: a sense of what to prioritise, what to do in what order, and what "success" would look like in five years versus fifty.

"We'd been paralysed by the scale of it. Matthew helped us see that you don't restore 30 hectares at once — you restore one small area really well, and work from there."

We worked through a set of questions together: What does this land want to become? What's the minimum intervention that will unlock the most recovery? What does the owner have the capacity to maintain long-term? The answers to those questions shaped the entire plan.

The Plan in Practice

The resulting management plan focused on three phases across the first three years:

  • Year 1: Invasive clearing in the highest-priority zones — particularly the wattle and rooikrans encroaching on the forest margin. Protect and expand the forest patch.
  • Year 2: Establish indigenous vegetation in cleared areas. Begin composting and soil-building in the zones earmarked for food production. Install water harvesting earthworks on the upper slopes.
  • Year 3 and beyond: Transition to maintenance mode in restored areas. Expand clearing to secondary invasion zones. Ongoing monitoring and adaptation.

What made this plan realistic was that it was built for a real person with a real life — not for a full-time conservation manager. The workload ramped up in year one when external labour was budgeted, and stepped back in year two when the owners could continue most of it themselves.

Embedding the Owner in the Design

A core part of our work at Six Kingdoms is making sure the people who live on a piece of land become genuinely interested in it — curious about the species coming back, attentive to how rain moves across the slopes, excited about what they're seeing. That relationship between a person and their land is what makes restoration stick long-term. Without it, the wattle comes back and the plan ends up in a drawer.

Two years in, this project is running well. The owners do quarterly walks with us and have started identifying plants themselves. The forest margin has expanded noticeably. It's working because the vision was theirs from the start.
