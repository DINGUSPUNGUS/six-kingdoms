Plettenberg Bay Pool Conversion

Plettenberg Bay, Garden Route — Conversion from Chlorine

Type Pool Conversion
Location Plettenberg Bay, Garden Route
Previous system Conventional chlorine pool
Outcome Chemical-free EcoPool
Running cost change Significantly reduced

This pool in Plettenberg Bay was doing what a lot of chlorine pools do: sitting there, running up a chemical bill, and not getting used nearly as much as it should have been. The owners weren't interested in managed chemistry — they wanted to swim without thinking about it. A conversion was the obvious answer.

Why Convert Rather Than Build New?

In the right circumstances, converting an existing conventional pool to a natural swimming pool is one of the most cost-effective ways to get to a chemical-free swim. You're working with existing structure, existing volume, and an existing site context. The main conversion work involves retrofitting a regeneration zone — either alongside the existing pool or by dedicating part of the pool volume to planted filtration — and replacing the chemical filtration system with biological alternatives.

This site had enough garden around the existing pool to allow an adjacent regeneration zone rather than a split within the existing shell. That's almost always the better outcome: you keep the full swim volume and add a dedicated biology zone that the water circulates through on its own schedule.

The Conversion Process

The first step was a full assessment of the existing pool structure, plumbing, and site. The existing pool shell was in good condition, which simplified things considerably. The chlorine filtration system was decommissioned, the pool was thoroughly drained and cleaned, and the water chemistry was allowed to reset — removing the residual chlorine load before the biology was introduced.

The regeneration zone was built adjacent to the existing pool, connected by submerged overflow channels. Indigenous aquatic plants were chosen for their biological filtration capacity and their suitability for the coastal Plettenberg Bay conditions: a combination of submerged oxygenators, emergent reeds, and bog-zone plants that together established a functioning biological filter within the first growing season.

"We'd been putting the pool off for two years because we didn't want the chemicals. The EcoPool sorted that. The frogs moved in by April."

Running the Numbers

One of the underappreciated benefits of a converted EcoPool is the ongoing cost reduction. No chlorine, no algaecides, no pH adjusters, no periodic shock treatments. The filtration is passive — circulated by a single low-energy pump running on a timer — and requires no chemical inputs. For a pool that was previously being managed with multiple chemical products through the summer, the saving is meaningful within the first year.

There's also the time saving. A natural pool in good equilibrium needs monitoring, not management. You check it, you enjoy it, and you get in. You don't balance chemistry.

Key Details

  • Existing pool shell retained and repurposed — no new construction required
  • Adjacent regeneration zone built using available garden space
  • Indigenous aquatic planting chosen for Plettenberg Bay coastal conditions
  • Chlorine system fully decommissioned — zero chemicals used post-conversion
  • Biology established within the first season; wildlife moved in by late summer
  • Running costs substantially reduced without the ongoing chemical programme
