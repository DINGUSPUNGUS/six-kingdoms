Knowing Your Land: The Foundation of Good Stewardship

January 2026 — 6 min read

Most land management problems we encounter have the same root cause: intervention before observation. Someone bought a property, noticed the wattles, and cleared them — without a plan for what came next. Or they planted a garden without understanding the soil or the water table. Or they tried to manage a problem species without understanding what was allowing it to spread in the first place. The results are predictable and expensive to fix.

The foundation of good stewardship is deceptively simple: before you change anything, understand what's there.

What "Knowing Your Land" Actually Means

It means spending time on the property in different seasons and different weather. Walking the drainage lines after rain to see where water collects and where it disappears. Noting which slopes dry out fastest in summer and which stay cool and damp. Finding the places where indigenous plants are persisting despite the odds — because those places tell you what the land wants to become.

It means identifying what's growing on the property, not just the obvious things but the ground layer — the grasses, the forbs, the seedlings trying to establish. It means knowing which plants are native, which are naturalised but benign, and which are invasive and need active management. On the Garden Route, that last category is substantial.

"The farm was in a bad way when Matthew first came out. Wattle everywhere, tired soil, not much happening. Two seasons later it looks completely different." — Johan van der Merwe, George

The Invasive Plant Picture on the Garden Route

The Garden Route has a serious invasive alien plant problem, and it gets worse every year without active management. The main culprits most landowners will recognise:

  • Black wattle (Acacia mearnsii) — dense stands that outcompete native vegetation, alter soil chemistry, and consume enormous amounts of water. Spreads aggressively after fire.
  • Rooikrans (Acacia cyclops) — particularly prevalent near the coast, highly competitive in disturbed areas and along drainage lines.
  • Pampas grass (Cortaderia selloana) — highly visible, fast-spreading, and allelopathic — it actively suppresses the growth of neighbouring plants.
  • Port Jackson (Acacia saligna) — another fynbos invader that forms monocultures and burns hot, promoting its own spread.
  • Hakea species — particularly problematic in fynbos, where their hard seed cases survive fire and germinate explosively afterwards.

Knowing which species are on your property, in what densities, and at what stage of establishment is essential before any management work begins. A few established wattle trees are a very different problem from a dense stand of mature trees with decades of seed bank in the soil beneath them.

Building a Management Plan That Actually Works

A good land management plan starts with an honest ecological assessment and ends with a realistic, prioritised action list — not a wishlist. There are always more things worth doing than time and budget allow. The skill is in sequencing them correctly.

Generally, you work from the least-invaded areas outward, protecting what you have before trying to recover what you've lost. This is counterintuitive for some landowners, who want to tackle the worst areas first. But starting with the worst areas often means high effort, slow results, and demoralising setbacks. Starting with the manageable areas builds momentum, creates refugia for indigenous species, and gives you experience before you take on the hard problems.

The monitoring habit

One of the most valuable things we help landowners develop is a monitoring habit — regular, consistent observation of their property. Not a formal survey, just a walk with open eyes. Noticing what's changed, what's coming back, what new problem is appearing at the edges. Most invasive plants are far easier to manage when you catch them early. A stand of young wattle seedlings is an afternoon's work. A mature stand is a years-long project.

The Role of Fire

Fynbos is a fire-adapted ecosystem — it evolved with periodic burning and many species depend on it to germinate and regenerate. But fire management on privately owned Garden Route land is complex. Prescribed burning requires permits, planning, and experience. Managed incorrectly it can cause significant damage and set back restoration by years.

If fire management is part of your stewardship goals, it needs to be part of the plan from the beginning — including firebreaks, fuel load management, and coordination with neighbouring properties and conservation authorities. It's not something to improvise.
